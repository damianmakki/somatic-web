## The Simplest Progressive Overload Plan You'll Ever Run

You've been pressing 80kg for about six weeks. The sets don't feel hard anymore. You're not sure if you should go up. So you do 80kg again next session, and the session after that, and the one after that. Four months in, you're still at 80kg. Not because you're not working hard. Because you've been accidentally running a maintenance program.

The American College of Sports Medicine identifies progressive overload — the gradual, systematic increase of training stimulus — as the single most important variable in long-term strength development.[^1] Not exercise selection. Not split structure. Not supplementation. Progressive overload, applied consistently over time.

Most people understand this. Far fewer apply it with the consistency the research says it requires.

## How Overload Breaks Down Without You Noticing

The most common failure mode isn't ignorance — it's inconsistency. Lifters know they're supposed to be progressing. But without systematic tracking, progression becomes a feeling rather than a fact.

Some weeks the weights go up. Some weeks they don't. Some weeks they go down because the session felt off. Without a clear record of what happened last time, there's no reliable basis for what should happen this time. The trajectory becomes a random walk rather than a deliberate climb.

The second failure mode is impatience — adding load before it's been earned. The rep range isn't hit. The form is shaky on the top set. But the weight goes up anyway because it's been two weeks and progress feels overdue. This collapses the system.

Both failures share a root cause: treating overload as a vibe rather than a measurable, trackable variable.

## Making Progression Systematic Instead of Approximate

**Set a clear rep target before adding weight**

Define your rep range and stick to it. If your working range is 8–12, the rule is: when you can complete 12 clean reps across all working sets, add weight and reset to 8. When you can only hit 8, the weight stays. This removes the guesswork from load progression entirely.

**Add the smallest increment available**

The smallest possible progress is still progress — and it compounds faster than most people expect. 1.25kg plates are among the most valuable equipment in any gym. Getting from 80kg to 100kg in 2.5kg jumps takes 8 sessions. In 1.25kg jumps, it takes 16 but fails far less often. Small, consistent increments win the long game.[^2]

**Track every session without exception**

Memory is not a reliable training log. You will not remember exactly what weight you used for your third set of Romanian deadlifts six weeks ago. Your phone will. Tracking creates the data that makes overload systematic rather than approximate.

Somatic automatically surfaces your last session's weights and reps when you open an exercise, and flags when you've been consistently hitting the top of your rep range — signaling that a weight bump is warranted. The progression logic is built in.

**Trust the trend, not the session**

Some sessions will be below your best. That's expected and irrelevant. What matters is the trend across sessions, not the variance within it. A bad session doesn't erase previous progress. Evaluate your progression monthly. Monthly trend lines are almost always more useful than session-to-session comparisons.[^3]

## One Lifter, Sixteen Weeks

A lifter runs a bench press progression using a simple 5×5 scheme. Starting weight: 70kg. Rule: add 2.5kg when all 5 sets of 5 are completed cleanly.

Week 1: 70kg. Completes all 25 reps cleanly. Adds 2.5kg.
Week 4: 77.5kg. Hits 5×5 on three of five sets, fails on two. Weight stays.
Week 5: 77.5kg again. Hits all 25 reps. Adds 2.5kg.

By week 16: 100kg. A 30kg improvement over four months. Not because any single session was extraordinary. Because the system was followed without exception — including the sessions where the weight stayed flat.

---

Progressive overload is simple. It is not easy. It requires tracking, patience, and the discipline to trust a process that produces results slowly and then all at once.

One rep. Every session. For as long as you train.
