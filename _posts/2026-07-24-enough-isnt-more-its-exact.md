## The Point Where More Training Becomes Less Progress

More is not always better. In training, past a certain point, more is actively worse.

This isn't an opinion. A 2017 meta-analysis in the *Journal of Sports Sciences* examined the dose-response relationship between weekly resistance training volume and muscle hypertrophy across 15 studies. The finding: volume and hypertrophy are positively correlated — up to a point. Beyond that point, additional volume stops driving growth and starts impairing recovery.[^1]

The problem is that most lifters have no idea where that point is. They add sets because adding sets feels productive. The pump gets bigger. The session gets harder. The soreness gets worse. All of it feels like it must be working.

<!--more-->

Sometimes it is. Sometimes you've crossed a line you couldn't see.

By the end of this post, you'll understand the three volume landmarks that define the difference between undertrained, optimally trained, and digging a hole — and how to use them to build a program that actually works.

## Why More Always Feels Like More

The "more is more" instinct is understandable. Early in training, almost any volume produces results. The body is highly responsive to novelty and stress. Five sets work better than three. Six work better than five. The pattern seems clear.

But this response diminishes with training age. As your body adapts, the same volume produces less stimulus. The natural instinct is to add more. And for a while, it works. Then it stops working, and the instinct is to add more still.

Dr. Mike Israetel and the team at Renaissance Periodization, whose volume landmark framework has become foundational in evidence-based training, describe this pattern precisely: lifters keep adding volume past the point of productive stimulus because the subjective experience of fatigue is indistinguishable from the subjective experience of effective training. Both hurt. Only one of them is building anything.[^2]

The result is a training program that looks serious on paper and produces diminishing or negative returns in practice.

## MEV, MAV, and MRV: The Three Numbers That Define Your Volume Window

**1. Understand MEV — the floor**

Minimum Effective Volume is the least amount of weekly training a muscle group needs to produce adaptation. Current evidence places this at roughly 10 sets per muscle group per week for most trained individuals.

Below MEV, you're maintaining at best. You might feel sore. You might feel like you worked. But the stimulus isn't sufficient to force the body to change. This is where a significant percentage of recreational lifters quietly live — consistent, hard-working, and chronically undertrained.

**2. Understand MAV — the sweet spot**

Maximum Adaptive Volume is the range where training produces the best results relative to recovery cost. Roughly 10–20 sets per muscle group per week, with significant individual variation based on training history, sleep, nutrition, and stress load.

Inside MAV, more volume generally means more growth — because your recovery capacity can absorb the stress and convert it into adaptation. This is where the bulk of your training should live.[^3]

**3. Understand MRV — the ceiling**

Maximum Recoverable Volume is the upper limit of what your body can absorb in a given week. Above MRV, additional sets don't drive additional growth. They drive fatigue, impaired recovery, and eventually, performance regression.

MRV feels productive in the moment. This is the core trap — high-volume sessions produce significant subjective fatigue that feels like effective training. But fatigue and productive stimulus are not the same thing.

**4. Find your personal landmarks**

MEV and MRV are not fixed numbers. They vary between individuals and between muscle groups. Start conservatively — near the low end of the range. Add one set per muscle group per week over 3–4 weeks. When performance starts to decline despite adequate sleep and nutrition, you've found your practical MRV. Back off and settle into MAV.

## Real-World Application

A lifter decides to run a high-volume chest phase: 6 sets of bench, 4 sets of incline, 4 sets of cable fly, 3 sets of dips. That's 17 sets for chest in a single session, repeated twice a week — 34 sets weekly. Well past most people's MRV for any single muscle group.

Week one: great pump, significant soreness. Feels productive.
Week two: performance stays flat. Still sore.
Week three: performance drops. Sleep getting worse.
Week four: shoulder irritation developing from accumulated fatigue.

Compare to a 16-set week distributed across two sessions. Performance climbs weekly. Soreness is manageable. Shoulder stays healthy. More growth from less work — because the work is actually within the window where it produces adaptation.

## FAQs

**How do I know which muscle groups are undertrained versus overtrained?**
Performance is the primary indicator. A muscle group responding well — strength increasing, pump consistent, soreness manageable — is in a productive volume range. A muscle group where performance is flat or declining despite consistent effort may be over or undertrained. Track both.

**Do these landmarks change over time?**
Yes. As you build training experience, both MEV and MRV tend to rise. A beginner can grow on 6–8 sets per week. An advanced lifter may need 14–16 to maintain the same stimulus. Reassess every training block.
